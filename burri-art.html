Burri made his first trip to Paris in 1948–49, where he saw the tar-paper collages of Joan Miró and figurative paintings with coarse, bituminous backgrounds by Jean Dubuffet. Upon his return to Rome, Burri began to experiment with his first signature material: pitch-black tar. He mixed it with heated oil paint, black enamel, and ground pumice stone to create the Catrami (tars), a group of around thirty works that date to 1948–52. They announced the artist’s method of working with one dominant material to create various textures and gradations of relief. He scored and gouged through the dense, sticky paste, and poured liquid tar into gooey puddles or forced it to dry in crusty patches. Burri chose a medium that originates in the bowels of the earth and exploited its primordial and infernal connotations. Tar and the related substances asphalt, bitumen, and pitch are derived from petroleum products and coal. Humans have used them for thousands of years: for mummification, to seal wounds, and as artists’ glazes. With the Catrami, Burri created a “material monochrome” that emphasizes the processes of accretion, layering, and blackout.

In 1950 Burri wedged two tree branches behind a canvas, causing distortions on the front. He gave the name Gobbi (hunchbacks) to this series in which the picture plane is bent out of shape and thrust into the viewer’s space. He soon began using curved metal rods to prod the fabric support. While at medical school, Burri had conducted his research thesis on rickets, a disease causing bone deformation, and he was familiar with kyphosis, or severe curvature of the spine. The Gobbi show physical distress, as the canvas threatens to split at the maximum exertion points. At the tips of the swells, the artist typically applied a film of glutinous synthetic resin: polyvinyl acetate (PVA), a product manufactured for adhesives and coatings. Burri used PVA—usually the Vinavil brand—throughout his career as a glue, sealant, fabric stiffener, and glossy varnish. In the Gobbi, it appears in blobs and thin membranes that mimic mucus or the pulpy edges of healing flesh. With their covert spaces and unsettling references to the body, the “hunchbacks” presage aspects of 1960s feminist art.

The Muffe (molds) are named for their visual effects rather than for a material per se. They display mud-colored lumps and layers of sludge that resemble bacterial invasion, soil, or excrement. “Mold” is the generic term for the many types of fungal spores that germinate in humid conditions. Burri approximated the look of mold by mixing ground pumice stone with paint, mineral particles, and synthetic resins. The clusters appear to fester in a state of simultaneous growth and decay, wreaking havoc on the painting surface. Cubist and Surrealist painters enriched the textures of their works with sand and gritty granules of pumice that disrupt illusionistic imagery. The fully abstract Muffe are more like dirt paintings: the agglomerations of pumice visibly weigh down the canvas without poetry or existential drama. Piled high in relief, certain Muffe bring to mind topographical maps or the arid landscapes familiar to the artist from his time in East and North Africa and the Texas Panhandle.

Burri was fascinated by the central role of white in Western art: the plaster intonaco, or smooth ground underlying frescoes; the gesso preparatory layer of easel painting; and the lead, titanium, and zinc white pigments employed for concealing and highlighting. In the Bianchi (whites), white comes into its own as color, process, and material. Burri illuminates our perceptions of white by varying tone, texture, and finish to create passages that are by turns creamy, chalky, opaque, translucent, warm, cool, glossy, and matte. He rarely used an artist’s brush, choosing instead to spread on the pigments with his fingers, a palette knife, or a trowel—the tool of bricklayers and plasterers. The Bianchi thus make visible the different actions of whitening or covering in white, whether on a canvas or a wall. Some Bianchi are composed in whole or part of cotton duck, muslin, or linen fabric. The textiles are found pieces of biancheria, or household linens—once the epitome of pristine white and now reduced to soiled rags, delicate skins, and patchwork fragments.

In 1950 Burri made his first Sacco (sack) from a cast-off burlap bag mounted on a stretcher. During his time in a Texas prisoner-of-war camp (1943–46), the artist had used found gunnysacks as canvases for figurative paintings. In the Sacchi series, the unpainted burlap functions as both support and ground. Form, line, color, and tone emerge from the textile’s warp and woof, stains, patches, and stitches. Burlap is produced with jute, a coarse fiber related to the finer linen used for artists’ canvases. As a result, the worn and tattered material looks like a traumatized version of a traditional canvas. When the Sacchi were first exhibited in Rome in 1952, one critic described them as paintings but “impoverished, rotted, consumed, and already wasted away.”

Burlap was ubiquitous on the front lines during World War II, and used for supply sacks, sandbags, and camouflage. The Sacchi, however, respond to the historical context of postwar Italy as an impoverished country dependent on the charity of the United States. A few of the gunnysacks Burri employed had carried foodstuffs from America’s Marshall Plan relief effort (1948–53), but most came from the flour mill in his hometown. The worn burlap attests to use by different hands as well as to the artist’s own laborious stitching. Lacerated and threadbare, the material evinces anger and shame but also vulnerability and dignity. The Sacchi find their closest parallel in the neorealist cinema of Vittorio De Sica, Roberto Rossellini, and Luchino Visconti, who captured the misery of the time with a similar weave of documentary fact, aesthetic strategy, and humanity.

Burri’s assemblages relate to the collage practice of Tactilism, which the Futurist F. T. Marinetti promoted after World War I in response to veterans’ experiences of blindness and shell shock. Informed by Maria Montessori’s methods for educating children in sensory discrimination, Tactilism emphasized the therapeutic role of manipulating materials and “hands that see.” Burri handled traumatized bodies as a doctor and understood the reparative force of touch. The powerful effect of the Sacchi results from a similar activation of the tactile sense. They compel the eye and hand to work in tandem as the viewer projects the feeling of the nubby burlap as well as the damage inflicted upon it.

In the mid-1950s Burri adopted materials essential to the building industry and to Italy’s postwar reconstruction. Instead of giving new life to fabric remnants, he repurposed pristine wood veneer, sheet metal, plastic sheeting, and insulation board. He also developed a new process of burning materials, which he called combustion. The Surrealists had invented soot drawing, or fumage, in the 1930s, but Burri was precocious in his mastery of fire as a full-fledged painterly technique. The Legni (woods) are composed of thin laminates, usually of birch or oak, meant to replicate expensive hardwood furniture and wall paneling. With the flame of an oxyacetylene torch, the artist buckled the golden-hued surfaces, painted brooding shadows, and carved jagged-edged holes. The veneer’s association with human shelter adds to the searing power of the charred passages. The Legni provoke instinctive reactions to fire: the desire to feel its warmth and ponder the multicolored glow braced by the knowledge that it can cause pain and destruction in a flash.

The Ferri (irons) of 1958–61 are made of cold-rolled steel (an iron alloy) straight from the mill. Modernist artists had previously assembled found-metal objects or welded iron into three-dimensional figures and abstract geometries. Burri’s innovation was to cut and join the sheets into austere reliefs, nail them to a support, and hang them on the wall as a form of industrial monochrome. Factory marks and inscriptions function as graphic and coloristic flourishes. The artist “stitched” the metal planes together with a welding rod and torch, forming seams of weld beads and slag. By varying the heat of the flame, he produced blue, brown, purple, and yellow tones (known as temper colors) on the steel canvases. The Ferri tilt out precariously and bear down on the viewer, vaunting their raw power and the risk involved in drawing too near. Shifting from image to material reality and from object to painting, Burri’s works evade any fixed reading.

As Italy’s era of reconstruction ended in the late 1950s and the nation experienced an economic boom, Burri began to experiment with industrial plastic sheeting. Distributed in large rolls, it was primarily used for packaging and tarpaulins. Each type of plastic adopted by Burri has a slightly different speed of combustion. The red and transparent works are made with polyvinyl chloride (PVC) and the black examples from polyethylene (PE). Using torches and lamps to melt the plastic, he draped and sculpted it; once cooled, it retained its new shape. He often encased the whole in another translucent membrane and fashioned visible air pockets, or voids within voids. Burri harnessed the material’s thermoplastic properties to create a new hybrid of the painterly and the sculptural with unnatural textures that make the plastic seem both inert and strangely alive. The Combustioni plastiche exemplify Burri’s means of finding beauty where one would least expect it—in mass-produced, disposable things.

Burri claimed that he “left nothing to chance” in his art, but he accepted the vagaries of organic and hazardous processes. His battery of techniques enabled him to rescue a composition on the verge of disintegrating, improvise a repair, or elaborate detritus into a painterly substance. The tension between chaos and control is most apparent in the Combustioni plastiche. Burri played with fire to astonishing effects, transforming the slick packaging material into something uncannily visceral, aided by his knowledge of anatomy. Many of these abstractions seem to turn the body inside out, as if probing into subcutaneous tissues and membranes. Begun in 1957, this series represents a radical use of tensile plastic sheeting as a painting support. The Combustioni plastiche debuted in 1962 at the Marlborough Galleries in Rome, London, and New York. The transparent works were displayed suspended from the ceiling (as seen on the first level of this exhibition) in Burri’s traveling U.S. retrospective of 1963–64.

The Cretti were inspired in part by the parched landscape of Death Valley; Burri and his wife, Minsa Craig, resided in Los Angeles during the winters from 1963 until 1991. But the series’ origins lie in a technique the artist had been exploring since 1948: inducing craquelure, the fine cracks one sees on aging paintings. The Cretti are made with a traditional artist’s pigment, zinc white, which forms a brittle paint film prone to cracking if not mixed with proper amounts of binder. Exploiting what other artists consider a limitation, Burri deliberately prompted some of the widest and deepest craquelure in the history of art. He encouraged the ground to fissure in patterns and helter-skelter episodes over a period of hours or days, depending on the amounts of water and binder in the admixture and the thickness of its application. In some respects the Cretti are self-making artworks that “perform” their compositions as they dry.

The Cellotex compositions, in contrast to the Cretti, are precisely determined. Burri had employed Celotex insulation board as a support intermittently throughout his career (the brand name is spelled with one l, while Burri used two in his titles). Now he scored through the surface, exposed the fibrous interior, and peeled back layers to form planes of barely perceptible relief. For the most part Burri worked in monochrome: with either the prefabricated ocher color of the fiberboard or layers of black paint. Many Cellotex were conceived as multipartite narrative cycles, each panel a different iteration of a given set of shapes. The late works revisit the geometric and biomorphic motifs and pitch-black color of the Catrami, affirming Burri’s retrospective view of his career: “My last picture is the same as the first.”

Nero catrame (Black Tar), 1950. Tar, oil, and pumice on canvas, 80 x 110 cm. Private collection. Photo: Alfredo Cacciani, Rome

Burri exhibited his tarry black pictures in Rome on several occasions between 1951 and 1954. Their rough surfaces are built up with uneven deposits of pictorial matter and clotted paint, in contrast to the smooth and planar monochromes of other modernist artists. The hard and resplendent mantle of Nero catrame hides an earlier, colorful painting below. The comparable “erasure monochromes” of the moment are Robert Rauschenberg’s black paintings made around 1951–53; the American artist visited Burri’s Rome studio in 1953 and subsequently covered several works in progress with newspaper and glossy dark pigment.

An anonymous poem fragment is scrawled in Italian on the stretcher bar of this work (translated here):

In the journey of night
only silence is heard
and only [illegible] is seen
and your heart,
echoes closely like a thought.

Glossary

Oil Paint

Paint made when pigments are mixed with oil (traditionally linseed oil). The oil dries into a hard film that binds the colored pigments.

Burri began painting with oil but later mixed the medium with other materials such as pumice stone and black tar. In addition to oil, Burri also mixed pigments with binders such as PVA, shellac, tar, acrylic, and vinyl.

Pumice Stone

Light, porous rock formed from lava. The material has been widely used in Italy since ancient Roman times and is still used in construction materials.

Burri used ground pumice for textural effects in his Muffe (molds), Bianchi (whites), Catrami (tars), and Sacchi (sacks) works.

Tar

Dark, viscous substance made from petroleum products, wood, or coal. It may consist of a mixture of hydrocarbons, resins, alcohols, and other compounds.

Tar can be glossy and sticky or dense and dry, properties that Burri exploited in various ways in his Catrami (tars) works, in which he used tar and tar-like materials like asphalt, bitumen, pitch.

Catrame 2 (Tar 2), 1949. Tar, oil, and corrugated cardboard on canvas, 43.5 x 50.5 cm. Vatican Museums, Vatican City. Photo © Vatican Museums

One of the first in the series, this Catrame is composed of geometric and biomorphic forms embedded in thick layers of tar and scaly impasto. The shapes reveal Burri’s debt to interwar modernism and its synthesis of Cubism and Surrealism. Yet the dense materiality combined with a nearly monochrome palette marks a departure from earlier abstraction. The scored patches and tactile contour lines make it look like a piecemeal collage rather than a painting. A conservation study undertaken for this exhibition discovered an underlying, multicolored landscape. When Burri first took up painting as a prisoner of war in Texas, he worked in a figurative style. It is likely that he created this earlier picture while interned. The canvas was one of the few that he chose not to destroy and shipped back to Italy via the Red Cross.

The conservation study undertaken on Catrame 2 incorporated infrared reflectography (IRF), a method of using cameras that are sensitive to short-wave infrared light to reveal layers beneath an artwork’s immediate surface. In this painting, IRF showed a landscape with a structure similar to the barracks at the prisoner-of-war camp in Texas where Burri was interned.

Rosso gobbo (Red Hunchback), 1953. Acrylic, fabric, and resin on canvas; metal rod on verso, 56.5 x 85 cm. Private collection, Rome

With the Gobbi, Burri began his research into what he termed “invasive space.” The ungainly protrusions in this work owe to the curved metal rod that snakes behind and prods the canvas. Two scraps of fabric glued flat to the vermilion-red field augment the visible stress to the support. On the far right, a rimmed red circle lies adjacent to the curved profile of the paint-dappled fragment. Together the two shapes resemble a painter’s palette with the characteristic thumbhole, in a subtle allusion to the Gobbo’s hybrid identity as sculptural painting. Burri’s “hunchback” canvases were widely publicized in a 1954 feature in ARTnews magazine.

Glossary

Acrylic Paint

Fast-drying, water-soluble paint in which the plastic vehicle is an acrylic resin.

Burri used acrylic and acrylic-vinyl paints in many of his works. In his Bianchi (whites) series, he knew how to achieve tonal nuances and an array of visual and tactile qualities by varying the relative proportion of acrylics, enamels, oils, proteins, PVA, and shellac binders in combination with white pigments.

Vinavil

Polymer emulsion similar to American household glues made by Elmer’s.

Vinavil was Burri’s PVA emulsion of choice. He adapted the white waterborne emulsion, which is flexible and dries clear, for a variety of applications when it first appeared on the market in 1952, including to bind pigments, stiffen fabrics, collage elements, and consolidate the forced cracking of the Cretti works.

Rosso gobbo (Red Hunchback), 1955. Acrylic, fabric, Vinavil, and combustion on black fabric; Vinavil can on verso, 86 x 100 cm. Courtesy Galleria Tega, Milan. Photo: Paolo Vandrasch and Romina Bettega

A black herringbone fabric with a vertical seam (at left) serves as the support for this work, which Burri covered with red paint. The swell and depression just above center together form a dark flower or an orifice with a mysterious interior. To make Rosso gobbo’s circular hillock, Burri took an empty can and secured it to the stretcher’s crossbar. Though typical of his ad hoc methods, its presence is telling; the can is labeled “Vinavil,” the artist’s preferred brand of polyvinyl acetate (PVA). The frieze of stained, puckered, and charred rags at the top of the picture adds a jarring touch of painterly exuberance. Burri added combustion, or burning, to his battery of techniques in 1955.

When viewed from behind, the inside of crushed Vinavil can displays traces of the red paint and transparent resin used on this canvas. By employing the can to form the hump in Rosso gobbo, Burri acknowledged the central role of Vinavil in the making of this picture and in his oeuvre as a whole.

Muffa T. (Mold T.), 1952. Oil, PVA, pumice, sand, and shellac on canvas, 90.2 x 110.5 cm. Godwin-Ternbach Museum, Queens College, City University of New York (CUNY), Gift of G. David Thompson, 1958. Photo: Kristopher McKay © Solomon R. Guggenheim Foundation, New York

The two-pronged motif seen in this Muffa appears in several early works from 1950–52. The shape is the closest that Burri came to rendering a linguistic sign or ideograph. It appears to push against the unformed matter engulfing much of the picture. The “T.” in several of Burri’s titles likely refers to the Pittsburgh-based art collector G. David Thompson, who acquired over a dozen pictures by the artist in the 1950s.

Thompson was an important early supporter of Burri’s work. In addition to being the initial owner of Muffa T., he owned a number of other Burri paintings that featured the initial in their titles. Thompson donated Muffa T. to the Queens College Art Collection in 1958.

Shellac was one of the many materials Burri used as a binder to mix dry pigments.

Celotex

Fiberboard manufactured as insulation and acoustic paneling for walls, roofs, and ceilings. Its characteristic fibrous texture also made it highly resistant to heat and easy to score and flay.

This support material allowed Burri to explore experimental combustion and textural effects in his Cellotex works (the alternate spelling was used to differentiate the series title from the brand).

Lo strappo (The Rip), 1952. Oil, fabric, thread, pumice, and Vinavil, 87 x 58 cm. Collezione Beatrice Monti della Corte. Photo © Christie’s Image Ltd

Here Burri cut through a remnant of household linen, meticulously finished the edges, laced it up with green-painted cord, and closed the ends with fabric tabs. The oily smudges and tide lines add to the work’s reliquary quality, giving it the character of a sudarium (a cloth for wiping sweat off the face). Burri’s compatriot Lucio Fontana acquired a version of Lo strappo exhibited at the 1952 Venice Biennale. The two artists engaged in a fruitful dialogue in the postwar years, as each violated the picture plane. Fontana had begun to do so in 1949 with small perforations; his slashed canvases appeared a decade later. Fontana’s cuts open onto blackness that suggests infinite space, whereas Burri’s lacerations remain insistently of this world.

Lo strappo is often presumed to belong to the Sacchi (sacks) series, but it is a textile construction made of a dirty and creased remnant of biancheria, or domestic linens. The oily or resinous, dark-brown stains reminiscent of tide lines on the right may be a testament to an earlier use of the fabric.

The title of this painting—“the rip”—exemplifies Burri’s tendency to name works for the method used to create them: its distinguishing characteristic is a long vertical laceration.

Bianco nero (White Black), 1952. Oil, enamel, pumice, and PVA on canvas, 65 x 100 cm. Private collection

Burri named his works by medium, procedure, and color, breaking with traditional narrative titles. As a general rule, when two or more colors or materials are listed, the dominant one in terms of area or visual force comes first. In Bianco nero, black threatens to overtake white and creates a dramatic and unconventional form of chiaroscuro (light-dark modeling). Induced craquelure was one of Burri’s signature techniques. He worked “lean over fat,” meaning that he reduced the amount of emollient binders and resins combined with his white pigments. As a result, the admixtures contracted and cleaved as they dried. Here the thick, battered whites sparkle and split to reveal the black strata below. Burri corrupted the old master technique of beginning with dark grounds and building up to luminous highlights, but achieved equally brilliant effects.

Combustione plastica (Plastic Combustion), 1958. Plastic (PVC), acrylic, fabric, staples, and combustion on canvas, 120 x 150 cm. Private collection, courtesy Sperone Westwater, New York

This work and its companion are among a small group made with stiff off-white plastic, which Burri used when he began his Combustioni plastiche around 1957. The heat of the oxyacetylene torch rippled and puckered the material, while the flame left long striations of brown and gray. Stretched and stapled onto a canvas support, this type of melted plastic resembles thick animal hide. Although Burri soon switched to thinner, more malleable sheets, he honed his combustion technique with these initial forays into the medium. Using his hand, a paintbrush, a stick of wood, or another tool, he distended and pierced the molten material while it was soft, forming high ridges and pulpy rims around the holes.

The choices for unconventional surfaces and supports expanded exponentially after Burri replaced canvas with abject burlap and pliable plastic. In 1966 feminist and art historian Lucy Lippard coined the term “eccentric abstraction” to characterize the work of a new generation of American Post-Minimalist and Process artists. They often manipulated nonart materials to achieve emotional expressivity, activated the viewer’s sense of touch, and represented the corporeal in disarming ways—as Burri did with the red Plastiche (and his earlier works). His training as a doctor and surgeon allowed him to evoke the recesses of the body where hands rarely venture, except in intimate or scarring situations.

Grande bianco plastica (Large White Plastic), ca. 1965. Plastic, acrylic, combustion, and Vinavil on Celotex, 191.8 x 292.1 cm. Kravis Collection. Photo: Tim Nighswander/IMAGING4ART, courtesy Glenstone

In the mid-1960s Burri developed the fifth subset of his Combustioni plastiche series: transparent sheets adhered to white Celotex, a commercial fiberboard resistant to heat. He overlapped the plastic in discrete areas, using the edges as subtle compositional divisions; note the horizontal line that runs along the top and the vertical drape near the center. Black acrylic paint fills shallow craters and channels, creating a sense of depth within the minimal relief. References to alchemy became a cliché in postwar art criticism, but the metaphor seems apt in Burri’s case. Here he used air, fire, water (to douse the flames), and earth (plastic derives from petroleum, a fossil fuel) to transform a mundane ready-made material into something refined. His mastery of the combustion process enabled him to make twisted and puckered strands that glisten like spun amber, copper, crystallized quartz—and gold.

Grande bianco plastica is made up of sheets of transparent PVC that Burri joined together using a torch and other implements, including his hands. Despite the apparent holes in its surface, a nearly invisible cover of thin plastic seals the entire work.

Burri’s transparent plastics defy conventions of recto (front) and verso (back). When they are suspended from the ceiling as he intended, light passes through these works, revealing the shaped volume of the painting and the space beyond it.

Grande bianco (Large White), 1971. Acrylic and PVA on Celotex, 126 x 201.7 cm. Private collection, United States. Photo: David Heald © Solomon R. Guggenheim Foundation, New York

Burri privileged the physicality of zinc white over its optical qualities. Here he allowed his paint mixture to dry in a lyrical, free-form pattern. Once the progressive cracking of a Cretto had run its course, or when the artist decided to arrest the process, he consolidated the surface with white paint or a coating of PVA or both. While the Cretti relate to techniques used in the earlier Bianchi, Burri was likely also aware of the Italian artist Piero Manzoni’s all-white Achromes series of the late 1950s, including the affectless works made by soaking fabric and other materials in kaolin (a white clay). Burri restricted his monochromes to pure paint, paradoxically using it to create sculptural relief.

For Grande bianco, Burri spread a thick layer of his zinc white and PVA mixture at the center of the composition and tapered the amount gradually toward the sides. As it was slowly drying, he scraped a trough down the central axis of the picture plane with a sharp tool and then smoothed the depression with his fingers. This furrow and the surrounding cracks evoke the spine and hide of a large animal.

Nero M 1 (Black M 1), 1988. Acrylic, pumice, and Vinavil on Celotex, 152 x 202 cm. Private collection, courtesy Sperone Westwater, New York

In the Cellotex, Burri experimented with a new type of flayed and painted black monochrome. Black paintings had particular currency in 1960s Minimalist art (notably those of Ad Reinhardt and Frank Stella). Burri installed his on black walls to augment the nuances of the color. In Nero M 1, he graded the picture plane in incremental steps, carefully slicing with a knife to form ridges in the textured paint. The deepest, velvety black areas contain pumice. As with the Catrami of four decades earlier, this monochrome hides lively colors underneath: tiny flecks of red and gold peek out from below, and pentimenti (traces of an underlying composition) appear throughout.

Subtle variations in the amount of pumice mixed into the paint in Nero M 1 create the gradations in gloss and hue apparent in this monochrome work. The intricacy of the surface is not readily discernible, but each differentiation reflects light in a different manner.

In his Bianchi (whites) series, he knew how to achieve tonal nuances and an array of visual and tactile qualities by varying the relative proportion of acrylics, enamels, oils, proteins, PVA, and shellac binders in combination with white pigments.

Celotex

Fiberboard manufactured as insulation and acoustic paneling for walls, roofs, and ceilings. Its characteristic fibrous texture also made it highly resistant to heat and easy to score and flay.

This support material allowed Burri to explore experimental combustion and textural effects in his Cellotex works (the alternate spelling was used to differentiate the series title from the brand).

Vinavil

Polymer emulsion similar to American household glues made by Elmer’s.

Vinavil was Burri’s PVA emulsion of choice. He adapted the white waterborne emulsion, which is flexible and dries clear, for a variety of applications when it first appeared on the market in 1952, including to bind pigments, stiffen fabrics, collage elements, and consolidate the forced cracking of the Cretti works.
